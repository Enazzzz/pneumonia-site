## Online Sources

### Website Article (CDC, Mayo Clinic, etc.)

**Format:**
```
Author Last Name, First Name. "Article Title." Website Name, Publisher/Organization, 
Publication Date, URL. Accessed Day Month Year.
```

**Example:**
```
Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. "Pneumonia." CDC, U.S. Department of Health 
and Human Services, 15 Mar. 2023, www.cdc.gov/pneumonia/index.html. Accessed 20 Oct. 2024.
```

## Print Sources

### Book

**Format:**
```
Author Last Name, First Name. Book Title. Publisher, Publication Year.
```

**Example:**
```
Smith, John. Understanding Respiratory Diseases. Medical Press, 2022.
```

### Journal Article

**Format:**
```
Author Last Name, First Name. "Article Title." Journal Name, vol. Volume Number, 
no. Issue Number, Publication Year, pp. Page Range.
```

**Example:**
```
Johnson, Mary. "Advances in Pneumonia Treatment." Medical Research Quarterly, 
vol. 45, no. 3, 2023, pp. 120-135.
```

## Why These Sources Are Trustworthy

When explaining source credibility in your project, consider:

1. **Authority**: Is the author/organization a recognized expert?
   - CDC, WHO, Mayo Clinic = High authority
   - Peer-reviewed journals = High authority
   - Personal blogs = Lower authority

2. **Accuracy**: Is the information current and fact-checked?
   - Recent publication dates (within 5 years for medical info)
   - Citations/references provided
   - Peer-reviewed sources

3. **Objectivity**: Is the source unbiased?
   - Educational institutions (.edu)
   - Government sources (.gov)
   - Non-profit medical organizations

4. **Purpose**: Why was this source created?
   - Educational/informational = Good
   - Selling products = Be cautious
   - Personal opinion = Use sparingly
