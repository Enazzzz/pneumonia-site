Signs & Symptoms

Pneumonia symptoms can range from mild to severe and vary by age and overall health. Early recognition is crucial for prompt treatment.

How someone gets it

Pneumonia is caused by bacteria, viruses, or fungi entering the lungs. The most common transmission method is through respiratory droplets from infected individuals.

  • Airborne droplets from coughs and sneezes (most common - approximately 65% of cases)
  • Aspiration of food, liquid, or vomit into the lungs (approximately 20% of cases)
  • Secondary bacterial infection following viral respiratory illness (approximately 15% of cases)

Source: CDC - Pneumonia Causes

Who is most at risk

Certain groups are at higher risk for pneumonia due to age, immune system function, or underlying health conditions. Understanding these risk factors helps guide prevention strategies.

Children (under 5)

Higher risk due to developing immune systems.

Elderly (65+)

Aging immune response and comorbidities.

Immunocompromised

Including chronic illness (diabetes, heart disease, COPD) and certain medications that suppress the immune system.

Diagnosis

Clinical Exam

Physical examination involves listening to the lungs with a stethoscope to detect abnormal sounds like crackles, wheezing, or decreased breath sounds. The physician also checks vital signs including breathing rate, heart rate, temperature, and oxygen saturation levels.

Source: Mayo Clinic - Pneumonia Diagnosis

üì∑

Chest X-ray

Chest X-ray is the primary imaging test for pneumonia, revealing areas of lung consolidation (fluid-filled air sacs), infiltrates, or pleural effusion. This visual confirmation helps determine the type, location, and extent of infection.

Source: RadiologyInfo - Pneumonia Imaging

üß™

Lab Tests

Blood tests measure white blood cell count, C-reactive protein, and procalcitonin levels. Sputum cultures and blood cultures identify the specific pathogen (bacteria, virus, or fungus) causing infection, enabling targeted antibiotic therapy.

Source: CDC - Pneumonia Clinical Signs

Treatment & Prevention

üíä

Antibiotics

Bacterial pneumonia is treated with antibiotics such as amoxicillin, azithromycin, levofloxacin, or ceftriaxone. Treatment duration typically ranges from 5-14 days depending on severity, patient age, and underlying health conditions. Early antibiotic treatment is crucial for preventing complications.

Source: CDC - Pneumonia Treatment

ü¶†

Antivirals / Antifungals

Viral pneumonia may be treated with antiviral medications like oseltamivir (Tamiflu) for influenza. Most viral pneumonias are managed with supportive care. Antifungal medications (fluconazole, amphotericin B) treat fungal pneumonia, which is rare but serious in immunocompromised patients.

Source: WHO - Pneumonia Fact Sheet

Vaccines

Pneumococcal vaccines (PCV13 for children, PPSV23 for adults) and annual influenza vaccines are the most effective prevention methods. The CDC recommends vaccination for all children under 2, adults 65+, and individuals with certain medical conditions or weakened immune systems.

Source: CDC - Pneumococcal Vaccination

History & The Future

Pneumonia has been a significant health concern throughout history. The 20th century brought revolutionary advances with antibiotics and vaccines, dramatically reducing mortality. Today, ongoing research focuses on combating antibiotic resistance, developing universal vaccines, and improving diagnostic technologies.

Key Milestones

The history of pneumonia treatment spans centuries. Before antibiotics, pneumonia was often fatal. The discovery of penicillin in 1928 and its widespread use in the 1940s revolutionized treatment. The first pneumococcal vaccine was introduced in 1977, and conjugate vaccines in 2000 further improved protection, especially for children.

Pneumonia has been recognized since ancient times, but significant progress came in the 20th century. The discovery of penicillin by Alexander Fleming in 1928 and its clinical use in the 1940s dramatically reduced mortality from bacterial pneumonia. The first pneumococcal polysaccharide vaccine (PPSV14) was licensed in 1977, followed by conjugate vaccines (PCV7 in 2000, PCV13 in 2010) that provided better protection, especially for children. Today, research focuses on combating antibiotic resistance, developing universal vaccines, and improving rapid diagnostic methods.

Sources: CDC Pink Book - Pneumococcal Disease, Historical Review of Pneumonia Treatment

The future of pneumonia treatment looks promising with several exciting developments:

  • Advanced Diagnostics: Rapid point-of-care tests that can identify pathogens in minutes rather than days.
  • Next-Generation Vaccines: Universal vaccines that protect against multiple strains and serotypes.
  • Antibiotic Alternatives: Research into phage therapy, immunotherapies, and novel antimicrobial agents.
  • Personalized Medicine: Treatment plans tailored to individual genetic and immune profiles.
  • AI-Assisted Diagnosis: Machine learning algorithms that can analyze chest X-rays and predict outcomes.

Sources: Nature Medicine - Future of Pneumonia Treatment, WHO - Pneumonia Research
